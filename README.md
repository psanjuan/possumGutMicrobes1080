> Abstract:<br>
Aerial toxin control operations are central to the management of destructive invasive
species globally. Despite their effectiveness, aerial operations invariably leave
survivors, individuals that re-establish pest populations and continue predation
pressure on native biodiversity. While survivorship following aerial control is well
documented, mechanisms underpinning survival remain poorly understood. Since gut
microbes mediate metabolism and detoxification in herbivores, we tested whether
exposure to 1080 (sodium fluoroacetate) during an aerial baiting operation produces a
reproducible shift in the gut microbiome of invasive brushtail possums (Trichosurus
vulpecula). <br><br>
Using 16S rRNA amplicon sequencing, we first characterised baseline gut
microbiomes from 81 possums sampled across a forested landscape prior to an aerial
control operation. Eight confirmed survivors were subserquently live-captured and
resampled to assess post exposure microbiome shifts (Experiment 1). Baseline
communities were consistent and dominated by Firmicutes, particularly
Lachnospiraceae and Ruminococcaceae. In contrast, toxin-exposed survivors
exhibited a pronounced microbiome restructuring, includinge reduced Firmicutes,
increased Bacteroidetes (Muribaculaceae/Bacteroidaceae), and emergence of
additional families (e.g. Christensenellaceae, Rikenellaceae), consistent with dysbiosis.
To test persistence of this exposure-associated shift, survivors were exposed 1080 in
captivity and resampled (Experiment 2). The microbiome signature persisted after
captive dosing, distinguishing exposed possums (survivors known, captive exposed)
from unexposed animals. Differential abundance patterns and a marked shift in
Firmicutes: Bacteroidetes ratios highlighted candidate taxa that may respond to, or
contribute to, toxin metabolism. Collectively, our results suggest gut microbiome
profiling may provide a practical biomarker of toxin exposure and help disentangle true
survivorship from reinvasion following control operations.
